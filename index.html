The Completed Naawi-Oodena

Source: CNN - Canada's largest urban reserve opens in Winnipeg (2023)

Naawi-Oodena ("Centre of the Heart" in Anishinaabemowin) is now fully realized as a vibrant 156-acre urban reserve on the former Kapyong Barracks site in Winnipeg. This $1.2 billion completed development represents the largest single return of urban land to First Nations in Canadian history, marking a significant milestone in reconciliation and Indigenous self-determination. The project was developed through a unique partnership between seven First Nations (Peguis, Sagkeeng, Brokenhead, Roseau River, Long Plain, Sandy Bay, and Swan Lake) and various levels of government.

The development, led by Treaty One Nation, is now home to over 8,000 residents and features world-class commercial, cultural, and community spaces that honor Indigenous traditions while providing modern urban living. The community has become a model for Indigenous-led urban development, incorporating traditional knowledge with contemporary design principles. The architecture throughout Naawi-Oodena reflects Indigenous values, with buildings oriented to the four directions and public art installations that tell the stories of the Anishinaabe, Cree, Oji-Cree, Dakota, and Dene peoples who call this territory home.

Source: Treaty One Nation Annual Report (2025)

Explore Our Completed Community

Residential Living

Our completed residential areas now house over 8,000 residents in a carefully planned mix of housing types designed to create diverse, inclusive neighborhoods. The residential districts were planned using Indigenous principles of community design, with circular layouts that encourage social interaction and connection to nature. All homes feature energy-efficient designs that incorporate traditional knowledge about passive solar heating and natural ventilation.

The housing mix includes:

  • 1,200 single-family homes with backyard gardens and solar panels
  • 900 townhouse units arranged around shared courtyards
  • 600 stacked townhomes with rooftop gardens
  • 500 apartment units (30% designated as affordable housing)
  • 200 elder-friendly units with accessibility features

All residential areas are designed with Indigenous principles and sustainable features that have reduced energy use by 40% compared to conventional developments. The neighborhoods feature extensive tree planting, rain gardens for stormwater management, and community gardens that provide fresh produce while maintaining food sovereignty principles. Each residential cluster includes a shared gathering space with fire pits for storytelling and community events.

Source: Naawi-Oodena Residential Report (2025)

Thriving Commercial District

Our completed 1.5 million sq ft commercial space has become an economic engine for the region while maintaining strong Indigenous cultural identity. The commercial district was designed to reflect traditional trading principles, with open plazas that encourage interaction and relationship-building between business owners and customers. The architecture incorporates traditional materials like cedar and stone alongside modern elements.

The commercial center includes:

  • 200 Indigenous-owned businesses ranging from tech startups to traditional crafts
  • 50,000 sq ft Indigenous food hall featuring traditional foods from across Turtle Island
  • 300,000 sq ft office space (95% occupied) including Treaty One Nation offices
  • 150-room boutique hotel with Indigenous cultural programming
  • State-of-the-art conference center hosting national Indigenous gatherings
  • Indigenous arts market with 50 permanent vendor spaces

The commercial district has become a regional destination, attracting over 1 million visitors annually who come to experience authentic Indigenous commerce, cuisine, and culture. Special business incubator programs have helped launch over 75 new Indigenous-owned businesses in the past two years alone, creating a vibrant entrepreneurial ecosystem.

Source: Naawi-Oodena Commercial Report (2025)

Vibrant Community Spaces

Our completed cultural and community facilities serve as the heart of Naawi-Oodena, providing spaces for learning, celebration, and connection. These spaces were designed through extensive community consultation to ensure they meet the needs of all residents while honoring traditional ways of gathering. The community spaces incorporate Indigenous knowledge about spatial relationships and ceremonial needs.

Key community assets include:

  • 35,000 sq ft Indigenous Cultural Centre with museum, archives, and language nests
  • Central Gathering Plaza hosting 200+ annual events including powwows and ceremonies
  • 20 acres of completed parks and trails with interpretive signage in multiple Indigenous languages
  • K-12 school with 800 students learning both Western and Indigenous knowledge systems
  • Recreation complex with aquatic center, gymnasium, and traditional games area
  • Healing center offering traditional and Western health services
  • Four ceremonial grounds for seasonal celebrations and spiritual practices

The community spaces have become hubs of cultural revitalization, with daily language classes, traditional craft workshops, and intergenerational knowledge sharing. The design of these spaces incorporates sacred geometry and traditional architectural elements like circular forms and east-facing entrances, creating environments that feel authentically Indigenous while serving modern community needs.

Source: Naawi-Oodena Community Report (2025)

Sustainability Achievements

Environmental Milestones

Naawi-Oodena has achieved its ambitious sustainability goals, creating what experts are calling a new standard for Indigenous-led ecological urban design. The development's environmental approach is rooted in traditional Indigenous values of stewardship and reciprocity with the natural world. The sustainability features were developed through collaboration between Indigenous knowledge keepers and leading environmental engineers.

Key achievements include:

  • LEED Gold certification for all major buildings, with three structures achieving Platinum
  • 100% renewable energy since 2024 through a combination of solar, geothermal, and purchased wind power
  • 90% waste diversion from landfill through comprehensive composting and recycling programs
  • 45% tree canopy coverage (exceeding target) with over 5,000 native trees planted
  • Geothermal district heating system serving all buildings, reducing emissions by 75%
  • Innovative stormwater management using traditional Indigenous techniques combined with modern technology
  • Urban agriculture program producing 15% of community's fresh vegetable needs
  • Dark sky compliant lighting to protect nocturnal ecosystems and traditional star knowledge

The community's sustainability features not only reduce environmental impact but also serve as educational tools, with interpretive displays explaining how traditional Indigenous practices inform modern sustainability solutions. Regular workshops teach residents about energy conservation, water stewardship, and sustainable living based on both Indigenous knowledge and Western science.

Source: Naawi-Oodena Sustainability Report (2025)
